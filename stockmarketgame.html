the Project

This effort grew out of personal experience with the app. My niece signed up for the semester long instance and asked for my assistance to pick her first set of stocks.

I started a list of the difficulties she encountered as she progressed through the game and devised a set of features and tools that would assist similar students across the network.

the Work

New features that promote general securities knowledge acquisition and access to more robust data came first. I followed with a set of prompts that would trigger players to stop and think about possible ramifications of acting and not-acting. Finally, I reskinned the app and added a night mode to save the eyes of teens trading late at night.

Home screen

A snapshot of relevant info. Students see where they currently rank; their overall return; and the total of their equity.

Not only will you see details on securities you currently hold, but you can choose to add unheld stocks to your watchlist.

Portfolio

This is the place to analyze your holdings, compare returns across asset classes, and sort stocks to look for trends.

You also get a bit of a dopamine boost as you see all those green lines (hopefully) move up.

Trading

Waffle charts over pie charts. 10% of your initial allocation of 100k must be spent in each of the three asset classes. The grid nature of waffle charts are much better at quickly seeing how they grow or shrink over time.

The trading flow is not much different from apps like Robin Hood, but I put a special emphasis on having eye-catching graphics to point out important data.

What stock do I search for?

One of the key pain points I discovered in the research phase was that students have trouble ideating what sort of stock to search for.

I fleshed out the search function to have curated collections of securities that are share a characterstic, to help students start thinking and analyzing categories of securities.

First Time Users

The Game is designed to be supervised by a teacher that has learned the materials provided by Sifma—but some schools don't necessarily have the time or budget to have a well trained staff member available for this purpose.

I created a robust first-time-user onboarding flow to mimic the instruction that should have taken place in the classroom.

Who do teens listen to?

My niece and I had several long sessions where we discussed key concepts and terminology. While I thought I did a rather decent job, I realized later that people explaining similar things on TikTok really captured her imagination in a way that I could not

I envisioned incorporating a curated selection of tiktoks from the community to help guide the students along with the app. They would rotate on a seasonal basis to help students stay abreast of emerging trends.
